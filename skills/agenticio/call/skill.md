# Call

## What Happens in the Eighteen Minutes After a Call Ends

You hang up. The call went well. You remember the key points. You made some commitments. The other person made some commitments. There were a few things you said you would look into.

Eighteen minutes later you are in the middle of something else. The call is still fresh but the details are already softening at the edges. Which date did they say they needed it by — was it the fourteenth or the fifteenth? Did you agree to send the proposal before or after they sent you the background document? Was it Sarah or Michael who was going to loop in the legal team?

By the next morning, you remember the tone of the call and the general direction of what was decided. The specifics are gone.

By the following week, when the deadline arrives or the follow-up is expected, you are reconstructing the conversation from memory, from the notes you half-wrote on a piece of paper you can no longer find, from a vague sense of what you think you agreed to.

This is not a failure of attention or professionalism. It is what happens when human memory meets the volume of conversations that modern work requires. The average professional has between eight and twelve significant phone or video calls per week. That is four hundred to six hundred conversations per year, each containing decisions, commitments, and information that is supposed to influence what happens next.

No one remembers four hundred conversations accurately. Call makes sure you do not have to.

## Before the Call: Arriving Prepared

There is a version of every call where you show up having done no preparation, relying on the other person to set the agenda and trusting your memory to provide the context you need. This version exists and it functions. It just functions at about sixty percent of what it could be.

The prepared version is different. You know who you are talking to and you have their relevant history in front of you. You know what was discussed last time and what was left unresolved. You know what commitments each of you made and whether they were fulfilled. You know what you want to accomplish in this call and you have thought through the two or three questions that will get you there.

When you tell the skill you have a call coming up, it pulls together everything relevant. Who the person is and your relationship history with them. Every previous call you have logged with them, summarized with key decisions and commitments. Any open follow-ups from those calls. Relevant context from your notes and emails if available. The stated purpose of today's call if you have shared it.

Then it helps you prepare. What do you want to accomplish? What questions do you need answered? What commitments from last time need to be addressed? What might the other person want from this call that you should be ready for? What would a good outcome look like and what would a bad outcome look like?

You get on the call knowing exactly where you stand, what you need, and what you are walking into.

## During the Call: Capturing What Matters

The fundamental tension of every important call is that you cannot simultaneously be fully present in the conversation and fully attentive to capturing it. Trying to do both means doing neither well. You miss things while you are writing. You write things down and miss the thread of what came next.

The skill resolves this tension. During the call, you feed it fragments. Not polished notes. Not complete sentences. Fragments. The kind of thing you would say under your breath while listening to someone: "deadline end of month." "They need the technical spec first." "John is the decision maker not Sarah." "They are open to the alternative approach." "Follow up Thursday."

The skill takes those fragments and builds them into structured notes in real time. When you say the call is done, it produces a complete record: a one-paragraph summary of what the call accomplished, every decision made, every action item with the person responsible and the deadline, every commitment made in both directions, and any open questions that were raised but not resolved.

You were present for the entire call. You have a complete record of everything that happened in it.

## After the Call: Making Sure Things Actually Happen

The call record is only valuable if it changes what happens next. Most of the time, what should happen next is that specific people do specific things by specific dates. The skill tracks all of it.

Every action item you committed to surfaces in your daily briefing as the deadline approaches. Every commitment the other person made is tracked separately, with a suggested follow-up drafted and ready if they go overdue. You never have to remember what you said you would do, and you never have to wonder whether the other person followed through on what they said they would do.

When the follow-up call happens, or when you send the email checking in, the skill has already prepared it. It knows the history. It knows what was agreed. It knows what is overdue. The follow-up message is specific, professional, and grounded in the actual record of what was discussed.

## The Call History: Memory That Actually Works

Over time, the skill builds something genuinely valuable: a complete, searchable record of every important conversation you have had.

This becomes useful in ways that are hard to anticipate until you have it. You are preparing for a negotiation with someone you spoke to eight months ago. The skill retrieves every call you have had with them, the commitments made on both sides, the things they said that revealed their priorities, the issues that came up repeatedly. You walk into the negotiation knowing them better than they expect you to.

You are onboarding a new team member and want to brief them on the history of a client relationship. Instead of trying to reconstruct that history from memory, you pull the call log and have a complete record of every significant conversation going back to the beginning of the relationship.

You are in a dispute with a vendor about what was agreed. You pull the call record from the conversation where the agreement was made. You have the date, the participants, and the exact commitments made on both sides.

The skill does not just help you manage calls. It builds an institutional memory of your professional relationships that makes every future interaction more informed and more effective.

## For People Who Live on Calls

Sales professionals who have eight to twelve prospect calls per day and need to track every conversation, every commitment, and every next step across hundreds of relationships simultaneously.

Executives and founders whose primary work happens in conversations, and for whom the gap between what was discussed and what was actually acted on is a constant source of frustration and missed opportunity.

Consultants and advisors who need to maintain detailed records of client conversations for both relationship management and professional liability reasons.

Anyone who has ever gotten off a call, known they needed to write things down, gotten pulled into something else, and then spent twenty minutes two weeks later trying to remember what was actually decided.

## On Privacy

Call records contain some of the most sensitive information in a professional's life. Candid assessments of colleagues. Negotiating positions that were stated in confidence. Personal information shared in the context of a trusted relationship.

Everything captured through this skill stays within your personal agent memory. It is never shared with any external system, any other user, or any third party. The call history belongs to you and is accessible only to you.

If you are recording calls or having calls transcribed through external services, the skill can work with that content. It does not require you to change how you currently handle calls. It works with whatever information you choose to give it.
